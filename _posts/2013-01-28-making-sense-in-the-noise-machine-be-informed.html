The first rule I try to follow is:

Be informed


Before Facebook, before Twitter, before Blogger and Wordpress, if you wanted to publish information you had to 1) stand in someone’s face and say it or 2) go through a traditional newspaper or publisher. The internet hasn’t just removed the face to face aspect of communication, it’s also taken away the gatekeepers (the editors and publishers whose job it was to keep poor content out of print.)

This has created two problems: First, you can publish whatever you want, no matter how ridiculous or untrue, and no one will proofread it besides your intended audience. Second, you can access a bunch of false or uninformed information and easily pass it on. The net effect of the internet and social media is that it has acted as a magnifier of bad ideas, misinformation, half-cocked criticism, and arm-chair-quarterbacking.

I mean, how did we ever know who to vote for before the advent of Facebook memes depicting Obama as one of the three stooges? How did we ever have a gun control discussion before NRA wallpapers or Youtube videos from liberals depicting “2nd Amendment Fails?” How did we have theological discussions without internet watch dogs pointing out ever misstep of some famous pastor?

We could all do with less uniformed nonsense on the internet. My pastor in college, Clarence Sexton, told us in chapel once

“If you want people to think you are intelligent, never talk about things you don’t know anything about.”


Yet the internet is full of people doing just that. To borrow from the Lincoln quote, they aren’t just being thought of as fools, they are opening their mouths (or laptops) and removing all doubt.

I try very hard to stay informed, and write informed things on my blog and my social media accounts. Here are some things I do to that end:

Read actual books and newspapers, not just blogs.


The internet is great, but for the reasons stated above, it’s not always the best place for disseminating ideas or truthful news. While, I’m grateful for the many wonderful blogs that I read, they aren’t quite up to the caliber of books. So before I’ll post on a political or theological topic, I try to have read at least one real book about it. Before I post on a news happening, I try to read an article about it from a reputable newspaper.

I’ve been reading seriously (at least 1 non-fiction book a week) for my entire adult life and I’ve got rules even for the books I’ll read. If I’m reading about politics for example, I try to read a book written by an academic (like Dinesh D’Souza or Thomas Sowell) and avoid books by Partisan commentators (like Hannity or Limbaugh.) If it’s a book about a theological issue or religious history, I try to avoid books written by people with an axe to grind.

Verify my references.


When I’m going to write or post on something, the last thing I want to do is pass on false information. If someone tells me something that’s sensational, I’m going to make sure I can prove it before I write about it. The internet makes this very easy.

An example: Last week I was meeting with a local pastor friend who had just met with a state senator. The senator told him about some legislation moving through our general assembly that could have serious negative effects on churches. While I totally trust my pastor friend, the first thing I did was look up the bill on the Illinois government web site and call the sponsoring State Senator’s office to ask them about it. After that, I called the local office of a non-profit that works with that issue and had a conversation with them about it. Then, I read as many newspaper articles as I could about it. Then, I wrote about it.

Consider the source.


Even if I’ve read a book or a newspaper article about something, sometimes, I still have my doubts. Some sources are better than others. Some sources are not to be trusted. Is the source of your information extremely partisan? (Like a talk radio host.) Do they have a vested interest in coloring their information a certain way? (Like a lobbying group.) Are they constantly passing on conspiracy theories or have they built their name through finding faults in others? While no news source is completely objective, I always try to consider if maybe this news is skewed by a preexisting agenda.

If I have nothing to add, I add nothing.


I’ve already mentioned this, but sometimes it’s just better to keep my mouth (or my keyboard shut.) As Proverbs says

“A fool uttereth all his mind: but a wise man keepeth it in till afterwards.” Proverbs 29:11


Quickly spouting off my half-formed opinion may seem tempting, but with the permanent nature of the internet, it’s a great way to do long term harm to my credibility.

Tomorrow, I’ll be posting about my second rule: be helpful. If you have any ideas, or you want to weigh in on this subject, then tell me about it in the comments.
