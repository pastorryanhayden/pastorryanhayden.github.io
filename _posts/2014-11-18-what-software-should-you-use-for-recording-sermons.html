Every church records sermons.  At least every church I’ve ever been a part of.  They’ve been doing it for years.  Still - one  of the most common tech questions I get is “what software should we use in the soundbooth?"

Here’s what you need that software to do:

  • Record sermons
  • Save to MP3
  • Basic audio editing (remove bad spots, clip beggining and end, amplify, etc.)
  • Be easy enough for volunteers to learn quickly.


There are a bunch of programs that meet the first three requirements, the last one however, is a little illusive.

Expensive Programs


The two expensive programs you probably will encounter are Adobe Audition and Apple Logic   (If you really want to get expensive, I’m sure Avid has something too.)  These programs are fantastic software programs - but they are aimed more at people running pro music studios than they are at Earl from the sound booth.  99% of what they do - you don’t need.  They have complicated interfaces and a learning curve.  Long story short - unless you have professional audio guys in the soundbooth and your church has lots of money to throw away, you should probably stay away from these programs.

Inexpensive Programs


One program I have used from time to time is a little app called KVLT.  KVLT is Mac only, available from the Mac app store for $10.  What I like about KVLT is that it is super simple.  It records audio, lets you see the wave form, and lets you save it to any common extension. That’s it.


KVLT is a great app to own, and in the past I’ve used it for making quick audio recordings.  But it’s probably a little too simple and because it’s Mac only, might not suit your needs.

Free Programs

Audacity

The most popular free audio app out there is Audacity   Audacity is a fantastic piece of open source software and is probably the program running in most church’s soundbooths.


Here’s what Audacity has going for it:

  • It’s Free.
  • It will run on anything.
  • It’s super popular - so you can find lots of tutorials on it.
  • It’s very powerful - it does things like multitrack editing and has many advanced filters.  These are things that you’d have to spend a lot of money on the expensive stuff above to do.


Here is what I don’t like about Audacity:

  • It’s ugly.  I mean really, really ugly.  The main buttons look like they were designed by Fisher Price.  In years of using Audicity, I’ve waited and waited for a redesign - but it never comes.
  • It’s intimidating.  Just like pro software, the vast majority of what Audacity does - your soundbooth will never do.  So it’s harder for non-tech-savvy volunteers to pick up.
  • It doesn’t do MP3’s out of the box.  You have to download and install extra software for that.  This isn’t a big deal, but it is annoying.
  • You can’t just hit Command + S to save to MP3, you have to export.  I’m sure there is a good reason for this, but I find it annoying.

Ocenaudio


My new favorite audio recording app is a less well known program called Ocenaudio   Ocenaudio does much of what Audacity does (although it doesn’t do multitrack.)  What makes Ocenaudio my favorite is it’s dead simple interface.  The app couldn’t get simpler - with just a waveform timeline, a row of good looking buttons, and a list of recent files (even that can get hidden.)  If you can run iTunes, you can run Ocenaudio.


Also, unlike Audacity, Ocenaudio exports MP3 out of the box, and you can just save the file into whatever format you want.  All of this translates into a program that volunteers from the soundbooth should have no problem with.

Ocenaudio is free and cross-platform (it will work on almost any computer.)  Check it out today.
